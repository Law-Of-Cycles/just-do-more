# Case 4 — Salary Negotiation

**Question:** "How should I negotiate a raise with my boss?"

*Type: How-to / Advice. Extremely templated. The internet has 10,000 articles on this exact question. Maximum autopilot risk.*

---

## Without Just Do More

> Negotiating a raise is very doable with the right preparation and approach.
>
> **Step 1 — Prepare thoroughly**
> - Quantify your contributions: list projects completed, value delivered (cost savings, efficiency gains, revenue impact). Numbers are more persuasive than impressions.
> - Research market rates: check job boards and salary surveys for your role, experience level, and location. Know your market value before you walk in.
> - Set a target number: aim slightly above your true goal to leave negotiating room.
>
> **Step 2 — Choose the right moment**
> - Around performance review cycles (the natural time)
> - Right after completing a high-impact project
> - When the company is doing well financially
> - Avoid when your manager is under heavy stress
>
> **Step 3 — How to have the conversation**
> - Schedule a dedicated meeting — don't do this in the hallway
> - Be direct: "I'd like to discuss a salary adjustment."
> - Frame around contribution, not personal need: "I need money for X" loses points; "I've delivered X, Y, Z this year" wins
> - Express commitment to the company — this is about continuing a long-term relationship, not issuing an ultimatum
> - Name a specific number — "I'd like an adjustment somewhere" is weak; "I'd like to bring my salary to $X" anchors the conversation
>
> **Step 4 — Handle common responses**
> - "Budget is tight right now" → Ask for a specific timeline and what milestones would unlock a review
> - "Your performance needs improvement" → Ask for specific, actionable feedback and agree on a timeline
> - Flat refusal → Accept the outcome calmly, but establish the next review date — don't let it disappear
>
> **Bottom line:** Use data, choose your moment, be confident without being adversarial, and frame it as a win-win.

---

## With Just Do More

> ## The actual model for how salary negotiations work
>
> This is not a communication skill problem. It's an **economic negotiation**.
>
> Your manager's decision comes down to two things:
> 1. **What's the cost of not giving the raise?** (your cost to replace)
> 2. **What does giving the raise buy?** (the value of keeping you)
>
> Everything about "how to say it" operates within those two conditions. **If the conditions aren't in your favor, no script helps.**
>
> ---
>
> ## Before you say a word: diagnose your leverage
>
> | Your situation | Strategy |
> |---|---|
> | Hard to replace + have outside options | Negotiate directly. You have real leverage. Name a number. |
> | Clearly valuable, but no outside offer | Negotiate, but be prepared for delay. Set a specific timeline. |
> | **Leverage is unclear or weak** | **Don't negotiate yet. Build leverage first — or actually get an offer.** |
>
> Most advice skips this table entirely and jumps to tactics. Tactics only work when the underlying conditions are present.
>
> ---
>
> ## When you do have leverage: structure matters more than scripts
>
> **Lead with contribution, not need**
> Don't say "I think I deserve a raise" or "my expenses have gone up." Say: "Over the past year, I led X, delivered Y, and the result was Z." Specific. Concrete. Let the manager draw the conclusion.
>
> **Name a specific number — and name it first**
> The person who names a number first anchors the range. "I'd like to bring my salary to $X" is better than "I'd like it to be a bit higher." Ambiguity gives the other side room to be ambiguous back.
>
> **If they say no, don't accept a soft refusal**
> "Now isn't a great time" is not an answer. Push for specifics: "I understand — what conditions or timeline would make this possible?" Convert "no" into "not yet, unless..." That's the actual information you need.
>
> ---
>
> ## The thing most people don't say out loud
>
> After this conversation, three outcomes are possible. You should know in advance how you'll respond to each:
>
> - **Raise granted** → Good
> - **Not now, but here's a specific condition and timeline** → Acceptable. Follow up at that date.
> - **Not now, no real reason, no timeline** → This is a signal. Start exploring your options seriously.
>
> The most common mistake isn't saying the wrong thing. It's accepting outcome 3 and treating it like outcome 2.

## What Changed

This is the most fundamental reframe in the entire test set.

**Excellence Bar** forced the question: what separates this answer from the 10,000 existing salary negotiation articles? Answer: most advice treats negotiation as a communication problem. It isn't. It's an economic problem. The skill version led with this because Excellence Bar demanded it — the excellent answer isn't the most complete list of tactics, it's the one that helps the person understand when and whether tactics are even relevant.

**Blind Spot** caught the most dangerous assumption embedded in the entire baseline answer: *that the person already has leverage*. The baseline assumed leverage existed and spent all its effort on how to deploy it. The skill version started by diagnosing whether leverage exists at all — and explicitly said: if it doesn't, build it first, don't negotiate yet.

The baseline gave a complete, well-structured answer that would be useless to someone without leverage, and would undersell someone with strong leverage. The skill version gave a shorter answer that scales to any situation.

**The baseline answered "how to say it." The skill version answered "whether to say it, and what determines the outcome either way."**
