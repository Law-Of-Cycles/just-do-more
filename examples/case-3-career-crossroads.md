# Case 3 — Career Crossroads

**Question:** "I've been at my company for 3 years and feel lost. Should I switch jobs?"

*Type: Career / Personal. Opinion-adjacent. High risk of generic framework or motivational platitudes.*

---

## Without Just Do More

> Three years is a genuinely sensitive time — this comes up a lot.
>
> I think the word "lost" is worth sitting with. What kind of lost are you? The answer points in different directions:
>
> **If it's "I don't know what I want"** — switching companies probably won't solve it. You might land somewhere new and feel the same way in six months. This kind of lost calls for figuring out direction before making moves.
>
> **If it's "I know what I want, but this place can't give it"** — maybe the ceiling is low, the direction is wrong, the culture is draining you — that's a concrete reason to leave, and you can move with real conviction.
>
> **If it's "things are okay, but I feel a bit burned out"** — that might be normal career fatigue, not a signal to leave.
>
> A few questions to ask yourself:
> - Is your state "I don't want to come in" or "I want to go do something else"? Fleeing is different from pursuing — pursuing is more stable.
> - If a company sent you an offer right now, would your first reaction be excitement or ambivalence?
> - If you're still here in three years, will you regret it?
>
> You don't have to decide now. But it's low-cost to explore the market — send some applications, talk to recruiters. The process itself often clarifies your thinking.
>
> What's the main thing you feel lost about?

---

## With Just Do More

> Don't rush to an answer — because "should I switch jobs?" may not actually be the question you need to solve.
>
> You said "lost." That word has two completely different roots, and they require **opposite** responses:
>
> **Root 1: The place is wrong.** Your growth ceiling is real, the work drains rather than builds you, the environment suppresses you, your market value and your compensation have drifted far apart. This kind of lost: changing jobs is the medicine.
>
> **Root 2: You are what's wrong.** You don't know where you're trying to go, so anywhere you are feels like the wrong place. This kind of lost: **changing jobs is just displacement. The feeling follows you to the next company.**
>
> Before you answer "should I switch," answer these three questions honestly:
>
> **1. Is your dissatisfaction about "this place" or about "me"?**
> Thought experiment: if your company moved you to a completely different project or role tomorrow, would you feel energized — or would it not really matter? If the latter, the problem is probably not the company.
>
> **2. Do you know where you want to go?**
> Not "somewhere better" — but a specific direction, role, industry, or even company that exists in your mind as a real destination. If nothing specific comes up, the impulse to leave is more likely escape than pursuit. Escape moves don't stick.
>
> **3. What did you accumulate in three years?**
> Not what the company thinks of you — what do *you* think? Skills, judgment, relationships, reputation. Did these three years make you meaningfully stronger? If that question makes you wince, your dissatisfaction is real and well-founded.
>
> Your answers to these three questions will tell you more than any "pros and cons of switching jobs" framework.

## What Changed

**Real Intent** identified that "should I switch jobs?" is a surface question. The real question underneath is: *is this confusion about the place, or about me?* These two have opposite answers — leave vs. stay and figure yourself out first. The baseline treated them as a single sliding scale. The skill version split them into distinct categories with distinct implications.

The phrase "the feeling follows you to the next company" — the most important thing to say to someone in this situation — only surfaces when you run Real Intent properly. It's uncomfortable, specific, and true. The baseline gesture toward it ("switching companies probably won't solve it") without committing to the idea. The skill version made it the diagnostic axis of the whole answer.

**Blind Spot** caught the "escape vs. pursue" distinction. The baseline mentioned it briefly ("fleeing is different from pursuing"). The skill version built a diagnostic question around it: *do you know where you want to go?* — not as a rhetorical point but as a concrete self-test with a meaningful answer.

**The baseline gave reasonable advice. The skill version gave a diagnostic framework that actually differentiates.**
