# Case 2 — Team Efficiency

**Question:** "As a team lead, how do I improve my team's efficiency? It's been consistently low."

*Type: Analysis / Diagnosis. Highly templated. One of the highest-autopilot-risk question types in existence.*

---

## Without Just Do More

> Team efficiency problems are common. Here are some genuinely effective approaches:
>
> **Start by diagnosing the root cause.** Low efficiency usually has a few distinct origins: unclear goals, high communication overhead, process bottlenecks, skill-role mismatches, or low morale. You need to know which one you're dealing with before prescribing anything.
>
> **Concrete actions:**
> 1. **Clarify goals and priorities** — What are the three most important things each person should accomplish this week? OKRs work, but even a simple weekly alignment works. The key is that everyone knows what matters most.
> 2. **Cut low-value meetings** — Cancel any meeting without a clear agenda. Anything that can be async, make async. Time-box everything, and end with explicit action items and owners.
> 3. **Build async communication habits** — Keep important decisions in written form. Don't let key information live only in chat logs.
> 4. **Delegate and reduce approval layers** — You're the lead, but not everything should go through you. Push decision authority to the right people.
> 5. **Protect deep work time** — Frequent interruptions are the most consistent efficiency killer. Establish "no-interrupt" blocks.
> 6. **Run retrospectives** — Not to assign blame, but to surface process bottlenecks and fix them iteratively.
>
> **One thing to watch as a lead:** Your own behavior gets amplified. If you send messages at 11pm expecting quick replies, the team can't focus. If you change priorities constantly, execution suffers.
>
> What specifically is showing up as "low efficiency" for you — low output, coordination problems, or something else?

---

## With Just Do More

> ## Start with one diagnostic question
>
> **What does "low efficiency" actually look like for your team?**
>
> "Efficiency is low" is an outcome, not a cause. At least four completely different root causes produce this symptom — and they require completely different interventions:
>
> | What you observe | What's actually wrong |
> |---|---|
> | Lots of activity, but little meaningful output | Goal / priority problem |
> | Everyone busy, but people blocking each other | Collaboration structure problem |
> | Individual execution is slow or inconsistent | Skill or motivation problem |
> | Direction keeps changing, work gets thrown away | **Decision-layer problem — possibly you** |
>
> Until you've diagnosed which of these you're dealing with, any "efficiency improvement" is a guess.
>
> ---
>
> ## Three diagnostic tools
>
> **Tool 1: Track where time actually goes**
> Don't ask the team where efficiency is lost — people's perception of their own time is highly inaccurate. Have everyone (including you) log actual time for five days at 30-minute granularity. The results almost always reveal: meetings consuming far more time than anyone realized, one or two people acting as information bottlenecks for the whole team, and large blocks of time spent waiting rather than executing.
>
> **Tool 2: Map where waiting happens**
> The most common real cause of low efficiency isn't slow execution — it's **waiting**. Draw a simple flow: from when a task is initiated to when it's done, what nodes does it pass through, and how long does it sit at each? A task that takes two days of actual work often spends ten days in transit.
>
> **Tool 3: Look in the mirror — are you the bottleneck?**
> This is the hardest diagnostic, but often the most important. Signs that the lead is the efficiency problem:
> - The team waits for your decisions, but your decisions are frequently delayed or reversed
> - You're involved in everything, but your calendar is already full
> - Team members won't move forward without asking you first
> - Final work products require substantial rework after you review them
>
> If two or more of these apply, fix your working style before addressing the team's.
>
> ---
>
> ## One counterintuitive recommendation
>
> **Don't add. Remove.**
>
> Most leads respond to efficiency problems by adding: more process, more tools, more meetings, more management overhead. This almost always makes things worse.
>
> The fastest efficiency win is usually identifying something the team is currently doing that, if stopped, would have no negative consequence within three months — and stopping it.
>
> Ask yourself: what are we doing right now that nobody would miss if it quietly disappeared?

## What Changed

This is the most dramatic transformation in the entire test set.

**Excellence Bar** completely changed the answer type. "How to improve team efficiency" has a well-worn template: enumerate frameworks, list tactics, add a caveat about diagnosis. The skill forced the question: *for THIS answer to be excellent, what needs to happen?* Answer: real diagnosis before prescription. A doctor who prescribes without diagnosing isn't being helpful — they're being efficient in the wrong direction.

**Blind Spot** surfaced the most important uncomfortable truth: **the manager asking this question might themselves be the source of the inefficiency**. The baseline mentioned this gently in one sentence ("your behavior gets amplified"). The skill version gave it its own diagnostic section with specific signals and said directly: fix yourself before fixing the team.

This is exactly what Blind Spot is designed to do — name the uncomfortable thing that the comfortable answer avoids.

**The baseline was a productivity blog post. The skill version was a diagnostic consultation.**
